Still above ECB 2% target after 3 years

Netherlands has not returned to the ECB's 2% inflation target since 2021. While the energy crisis has passed, services inflation — driven by wage growth, housing costs, and sticky domestic prices — keeps overall inflation at 2.5–4%. Housing rents accelerated to 5.1% in 2025, a growing concern for households.

Phase 1 · 2020
COVID Demand Collapse
1.1%
Lockdowns crushed consumer demand. Energy prices fell sharply (electricity & gas: -12% in 2020). The lowest inflation in a decade — a false calm before the storm.
Phase 2 · 2021
Supply Chain Shock
2.8% → 6.4% (Dec)
Reopening economies clashed with broken supply chains. Energy surged +17.6% as economies reopened. By December 2021, the warning signs were clear: inflation was accelerating fast.
Phase 3 · 2022
🔥 Energy Crisis
Peak: 17.1%
Russia invaded Ukraine in Feb 2022. Dutch TTF gas prices hit all-time highs. Electricity & gas spiked +114% for the year. Netherlands was harder hit than Germany (8.7%) due to gas reliance and Groningen phase-out.
Phase 4 · 2023
Dramatic Disinflation
17.1% → -1%
Energy collapsed -37% as warm winter, LNG imports, and demand destruction kicked in. Brief deflation in Sep–Oct 2023. But food inflation hit its own peak at 11.9%, hitting household budgets hardest.
Phase 5 · 2024–2025
Sticky Normalization
3.0–3.2%
Energy normalized but services refused to cool. Housing rents hit 5.1% in 2025 — a structural issue as Netherlands faces housing shortage. Wage growth supports purchasing power but keeps services inflation elevated.
⚖️ Vs. Neighbors
Netherlands Outlier
NL: 3.0% · EU: 2.5%
In 2025, NL inflation (3.0%) exceeded Germany (2.3%) and the EU average (2.5%). Persistent housing cost pressure and strong wage growth keep Dutch inflation structurally higher than peer economies.
